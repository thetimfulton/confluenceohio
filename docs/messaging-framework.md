# Confluence Ohio — Messaging Framework

---

## 1. Mission Statement

We are building a civic movement to rename Columbus, Ohio to Confluence, Ohio — replacing a name honoring a man who never set foot in this state with one that describes the meeting of rivers, peoples, and ideas that made this city possible.

*(29 words)*

---

## 2. Vision Statement

A city whose name tells the truth about what it is: Ohio's largest, most diverse, fastest-growing metropolis — a place defined not by a 15th-century colonizer but by what actually happens here. When Confluence, Ohio appears on the map, every resident sees a name that belongs to them, rooted in the geography beneath their feet and the convergence of cultures that makes this city extraordinary. The renaming becomes a model for how American communities can choose their own names — not through anger or erasure, but through honest conversation and democratic action.

## 3. Campaign Manifesto

**Where the Rivers Meet**

Two rivers made this city.

The Scioto — 231 miles of water whose Wyandot name likely means "deer," the river that appears on Ohio's own seal — flows south from its headwaters near Kenton through the heart of the state. The Olentangy — whose name was accidentally swapped onto it by the Ohio General Assembly in 1833, when legislators confused it with another creek — winds down from Delaware County through university campuses and park lands. They meet just northwest of where North Bank Park sits today, in the northern edge of downtown.

That meeting of waters is why we are here.

In 1812, when the Ohio legislature went looking for a site for its new capital, they chose this spot — "High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto," as the founding document reads. The rivers gave this place its reason. The confluence of the Scioto and the Olentangy was not just convenient geography; it was a crossroads that Indigenous peoples — Shawnee, Delaware (Lenape), Mingo, and Wyandot among them — had recognized for centuries. Nearly two hundred burial and ceremonial mounds once dotted Franklin County, many of them clustered near the rivers' meeting point. Long before any European arrived, this was a place of convergence.

The city that grew here inherited that character. Columbus is Ohio's largest city, its youngest major metro, and one of America's fastest growing. It is a place where Somali immigrants and Appalachian migrants live as neighbors, where research universities and logistics hubs sit side by side, where the biggest city in a swing state wrestles with questions the whole country is asking. This city is defined by what comes together here — not by what one man did five centuries ago on Caribbean islands three time zones away.

About that name. In 1812, a Franklinton tavern owner and state legislator named Joseph Foos reportedly convinced his colleagues — legend has it, over drinks at his establishment — to name the new capital after Christopher Columbus. It was a popular choice for its era; Columbus was widely admired in the early American republic as a symbol of exploration and discovery. Foos could not have known what we know now — that Columbus enslaved hundreds of Arawak people, that he governed so brutally the Spanish Crown imprisoned him, that the romantic mythology around him would begin to crumble under historical scrutiny.

But here is the thing we want to say clearly: this is not fundamentally about whether Christopher Columbus was a good or bad person. Historians can debate his full legacy. Our argument is simpler. Columbus — the explorer — has no connection to Columbus — the city. He never set foot on the North American continent. He never saw the Scioto, never stood at the confluence, never knew this place existed. His name was borrowed as a gesture of admiration for a distant figure. It was a fine name for 1812. It does not have to be our name forever.

We think "Confluence" tells a truer story.

It is the literal geographic fact that created the city. It is a word that describes what the city does every day — bring different people, industries, traditions, and ideas into the same space. It is distinctive: no other major American city carries the name. It honors no single person, no single culture, no single moment in time. It belongs to everyone who lives here.

We know this is a big ask. Two centuries of tradition carry real weight. The name "Columbus" is woven into signs, sports teams, songs, and the daily speech of nearly a million people. We respect that attachment, and we are not here to mock it or dismiss it. We are here to start a conversation — through the democratic process that Ohio law provides — about whether this city's name could better reflect the city itself.

The rivers are still meeting. They have been meeting since long before 1812, and they will keep meeting long after every one of us is gone.

We think the name should say so.

## 4. Name Origin Story

### Why "Confluence"

**The literal geography.** Columbus, Ohio exists because two rivers meet here. The Scioto River — 231 miles long, the longest river flowing entirely within Ohio's borders, prominent enough to appear on the state seal — runs south through the center of the state. The Olentangy River — 97 miles long, its name a product of a legislative mix-up in 1833 when the Ohio General Assembly accidentally reassigned a Delaware (Lenape) name meaning "river of the red face paint" from its original waterway (likely Big Darby Creek) to the river the settlers had been calling "Whetstone" (from the Delaware *keenhongsheconsepung*, meaning "place of the sharp tool") — joins it in the northern reaches of downtown, just northwest of present-day North Bank Park.

This confluence is not incidental to the city's founding — it is the reason for it. When Ohio's legislature selected a site for the new state capital in 1812, they chose specifically "the High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto." The rivers determined the city's location. The geography came first; everything else followed.

**The Indigenous precedent.** Long before European settlers arrived, this confluence was a recognized gathering place. The Shawnee, Delaware (Lenape), Mingo, and Wyandot peoples all had presence in the area. Franklin County once held nearly 200 burial and ceremonial mounds — earthworks that speak to centuries of spiritual and civic significance at this meeting of waters. The rivers' names themselves carry Indigenous roots: *Scioto* from the Wyandot language, likely meaning "deer" (reflecting the abundant wildlife along its banks), and the Olentangy's original Delaware designation pointing to the rich mineral deposits that drew people to these waterways.

**The metaphorical resonance.** What was true geographically in 1812 is true culturally today. Columbus is one of the most demographically diverse cities in the Midwest. It is a meeting point of Appalachian and African American and Somali and Nepali and Latin American communities. It is where Ohio State's research enterprise converges with one of America's largest logistics sectors, where state government meets grassroots organizing, where a city of 905,000 serves as the economic and cultural center of a metro area of 2.2 million. "Confluence" does not just describe the rivers — it describes the city.

**The contrast with "Columbus."** Christopher Columbus never reached the North American continent. His voyages brought him to Caribbean islands, Central America, and the coast of South America — but never anywhere near Ohio. The name was chosen in 1812 by state legislators who admired the explorer as a symbol of discovery. That admiration made sense in context; it does not make the name a natural fit for a 21st-century Ohio city. "Confluence" replaces a borrowed tribute to a distant historical figure with a word that describes what this city actually is and has always been.

**The branding opportunity.** No other major American city is named Confluence. (A small borough of roughly 750 people in Somerset County, Pennsylvania carries the name — a place, incidentally, that is also named for the meeting of rivers.) The name is distinctive, memorable, immediately evocative, and phonetically strong. "Confluence, Ohio" tells you something about the place the moment you hear it.

## 5. The Case for Renaming — Seven Core Arguments

### Argument 1: Christopher Columbus's documented legacy does not merit civic honor

The historical record on Columbus is not ambiguous. On his second voyage in 1493, Columbus enslaved an estimated 550 Arawak people, roughly 200 of whom died during the transatlantic crossing. As governor of Hispaniola, he imposed a gold tribute system under which Indigenous people who failed to meet quotas had their hands cut off. Forced labor in mines killed roughly a third of the native workforce every six months, according to contemporary accounts. His governance was so brutal that the Spanish Crown sent Francisco de Bobadilla to arrest him in 1500; Columbus was returned to Spain in chains. Bartolomé de Las Casas, a priest who witnessed the aftermath, described the conditions as systematic destruction of entire peoples.

This is not revisionist history — these are facts documented in Spanish colonial records and contemporaneous accounts. A city need not tear down every historical reference, but naming a state capital after someone with this record is an active civic choice that can be reconsidered.

**Source note:** Primary accounts from Las Casas's *A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies* (1552); Columbus's own letters and logs; Bobadilla's report to the Spanish Crown.

### Argument 2: Columbus has zero connection to Ohio

Christopher Columbus never set foot on the North American continent. His four voyages (1492–1504) took him to the Caribbean, Central America, and the northern coast of South America. He never saw the Scioto River, never knew the confluence existed, never came within a thousand miles of Ohio. The name was chosen in 1812 as a tribute to a figure admired in the early republic — not because of any connection to this place. "Confluence" replaces borrowed admiration with geographic truth.

### Argument 3: The city itself has already acknowledged the problem

Columbus, Ohio has taken its own steps to distance itself from its namesake:

- **July 1, 2020:** Mayor Andrew Ginther ordered the removal of a 20-foot Christopher Columbus statue from outside City Hall. The statue, erected in 1955, was placed in storage at a cost of approximately $90,000. The city has confirmed it will not be reinstalled.
- **2018–2020:** The city stopped officially celebrating Columbus Day and transitioned to recognizing Indigenous Peoples' Day. (Ohio at the state level later created a dual "Columbus Day / Indigenous Peoples' Day" designation.)
- **June 2023:** The city launched the "Reimagining Columbus" initiative — a $3.5 million project ($2 million from the Mellon Foundation's Monuments Project, $1.5 million from the City of Columbus) to rethink the space where the statue stood and the broader question of how the city tells its own story. The initiative runs through June 2025.

When a city removes the statue, stops celebrating the holiday, and spends $3.5 million reimagining the narrative — the name is the remaining question.

### Argument 4: "Confluence" is geographically accurate and place-based

The name describes the physical feature that caused the city to exist. The Scioto and Olentangy rivers meet in the northern reach of downtown Columbus — a fact recognized in the city's founding document and visible today from the bridges and parks that line the riverfront. Place-based names root a city in its actual geography rather than in a figure from a different continent and century. Cities named for their geography — from Portland to Salt Lake City to the Twin Cities — carry an inherent authenticity that borrowed names do not.

### Argument 5: The name tells a truer story about the city's identity

Columbus is defined by convergence. It is one of the most diverse cities in the Midwest — home to one of the largest Somali communities in the United States, significant Nepali and Bhutanese refugee populations, historic African American and Appalachian communities, and a growing Latin American presence. It is where Ohio State University's research enterprise meets a logistics and distribution sector that makes the city a national shipping hub. It is a state capital and a college town and an emerging tech center simultaneously. "Confluence" captures that identity in a single word. "Columbus" tells you about a 15th-century Italian mariner.

### Argument 6: A distinctive branding opportunity

No other major American city is named Confluence. The name is immediately evocative, easy to spell, and carries positive connotations: coming together, meeting, convergence. In an era when cities compete for talent, investment, and attention, a distinctive name is an asset. "Columbus" is shared with Columbus, Georgia; Columbus, Indiana; Columbus, Mississippi; and dozens of other places. "Confluence" would be singular. It would generate national and international media attention. It would signal a city confident enough to choose its own name.

### Argument 7: Precedent exists — cities have done this before

American cities have renamed themselves throughout history, and the sky has not fallen:

- **Utqiaġvik, Alaska (2016):** Formerly Barrow. Residents voted 381–375 on October 10, 2016 to restore the original Iñupiaq name meaning "place for gathering wild roots." The change took effect December 1, 2016.
- **Cincinnati, Ohio (1790):** Originally Losantiville, a somewhat tortured neologism coined by surveyor John Filson. Governor Arthur St. Clair renamed it Cincinnati on January 4, 1790 — right here in Ohio.
- **Atlanta, Georgia (1845):** Originally Terminus (1837), then Marthasville (1843), before becoming Atlanta in 1845.
- **St. Paul, Minnesota (1849):** The settlement was informally known as "Pig's Eye Landing" after Pierre Parrant, a one-eyed trader and tavern owner. A Catholic priest established a chapel and named it St. Paul; the city followed.

Internationally, Bombay became Mumbai (officially in 1995–96) as part of a broader movement to shed colonial-era names, with no lasting negative impact on the city's global stature.

Cities change their names when the old name no longer fits. The process is disruptive and then it is normal.

## 6. Steelman of the Opposition — Seven Counterarguments, Honestly Presented

We believe in making the strongest possible case for the other side. Here are the seven best arguments against renaming — and we take each of them seriously.

### Counterargument 1: Two centuries of tradition carry real weight

"Columbus" has been the city's name since 1812 — over 210 years. Generations of residents have built their lives, raised families, started businesses, and identified as Columbusites under this name. Civic identity is not trivial. Names accrue meaning through lived experience, not just etymology. For many residents, "Columbus" means *their* city — Saturday mornings in German Village, summer nights at Huntington Park, the feeling of saying "I'm from Columbus" — and that personal connection is entirely separate from a 15th-century explorer.

**Our response:** We agree that tradition has value. We are not dismissing 210 years of history — we are asking whether the next 210 years might be better served by a name that describes this place rather than honoring a man with no connection to it. The lived experience of Columbus does not disappear with a new name; it becomes the foundation for what comes next.

### Counterargument 2: The cost and logistical complexity would be enormous

Renaming a city of 905,000 people means changing road signs, government documents, official stationery, building names, vehicle markings, the airport (John Glenn Columbus International), legal filings, business registrations, and more. Conservative estimates for a renaming of this scale run into the tens of millions of dollars, spread across city, county, state, and federal entities and the private sector.

**Our response:** This is a legitimate concern and we will not minimize it. The cost is real. However, cities routinely update signage, documents, and systems over time — replacing signs at end-of-life, reprinting documents on schedule, updating digital records in bulk. A phased approach spread over several years — as Utqiaġvik implemented — reduces the shock. The "Reimagining Columbus" initiative has already committed $3.5 million to rethinking the city's relationship with its name. And some costs are investments: a distinctive new brand generates economic attention that partially offsets transition expenses.

### Counterargument 3: Brand recognition loss

"Columbus, Ohio" is nationally recognized. It is the 14th-largest city in the United States. Businesses, universities, and institutions have built brands around the name. The Columbus Crew, Columbus Blue Jackets, and Columbus Clippers are known commodities. Throwing away that brand equity is a real economic risk.

**Our response:** We take this seriously. However, Mumbai did not lose global recognition when it changed from Bombay. Brand equity follows the city's substance — its economy, its institutions, its people — not the specific string of letters. OSU will still be in Columbus (or Confluence). The Short North will still be the Short North. And the name change itself would generate an extraordinary amount of earned media and national attention — a branding event, not a branding loss.

### Counterargument 4: Confusion during transition

Mail, GPS systems, databases, legal documents, maps — a rename creates a transition period where both names are in use and confusion is inevitable. This affects residents, businesses, and anyone trying to find or reference the city.

**Our response:** True, and any implementation plan must account for it with a generous transition period, dual-name handling (as Mumbai/Bombay maintained for years), and a systematic rollout. The USPS and mapping services have established processes for name changes. It is disruptive, and then it is normal.

### Counterargument 5: Italian American heritage connection

For Italian Americans, Christopher Columbus has historically represented a point of pride and cultural visibility in a country that discriminated against Italian immigrants. Columbus Day was established in part as recognition of Italian American contributions. Renaming the city can feel like an erasure of that heritage.

**Our response:** We understand this, and we want to be direct: the intention is not to erase Italian American heritage. Italian American contributions to Columbus are real and significant — from the markets of the old North End to present-day institutions. The question is whether one historical figure's name on a city is the right vehicle for that recognition. We would actively support dedicated Italian American heritage recognition — a cultural center, a heritage trail, named spaces — that celebrate the community's actual contributions to this city, rather than tethering that recognition to a figure whose legacy is increasingly contested.

### Counterargument 6: The name has been reclaimed — "Columbus" means the city, not the man

Many residents have no mental association between the city name and the historical explorer. "Columbus" simply means home. The name has taken on its own identity, fully detached from its origin. Renaming imposes a political meaning on a word that most people experience as apolitical.

**Our response:** This is perhaps the strongest objection, and we respect it. Names do evolve beyond their origins. But the fact that the city itself felt compelled to remove the statue, replace the holiday, and spend $3.5 million "reimagining" the name's legacy suggests the connection is not as fully severed as it might seem. If the name truly had no connection to the man, those actions would not have been necessary.

### Counterargument 7: The slippery slope — where does it end?

Over 6,000 U.S. places bear names connected to Christopher Columbus — including the District of Columbia. If Columbus, Ohio renames, does that mean every Columbus, every Columbia, every place with a contested name must change? Where is the line?

**Our response:** Each community decides for itself. Columbus, Ohio's decision would not compel any other city to do anything. Ohio law provides a clear democratic mechanism — a citizen petition and a public vote — for this specific city to make this specific choice. That other places might ask the same question is not an argument against asking it here. Democracy is not a slippery slope.

## 7. Frequently Asked Questions

### The Basics

**Q1: What is Confluence Ohio?**
Confluence Ohio is a civic campaign to rename Columbus, Ohio to Confluence, Ohio — replacing a name honoring a historical figure with no connection to this place with one rooted in the geography that created the city. The Scioto and Olentangy rivers meet in downtown Columbus; that confluence is why the city exists.

**Q2: Why "Confluence" specifically?**
Confluence is the geographic term for the meeting of two rivers — which is exactly what happens in downtown Columbus. The Scioto and Olentangy converge just northwest of North Bank Park. This is the physical feature that determined the city's location when the state legislature chose it as the capital in 1812. The word also captures the city's identity as a place where diverse communities, industries, and ideas come together. No other major American city carries this name.

**Q3: Is this a joke, like the Flavortown petition?**
No. The 2020 Flavortown petition — which collected over 117,000 signatures — demonstrated that tens of thousands of people were genuinely open to rethinking the city's name. We channel that energy into a substantive proposal: a name rooted in geography and history, pursued through the legal process Ohio provides.

**Q4: Who is behind this campaign?**
Confluence Ohio is a 501(c)(4) civic organization led by Columbus residents. We are transparent about our team, our funding, and our methods. See our About page for details.

### The Process

**Q5: How would this actually happen legally?**
Under Columbus's city charter, citizens can propose charter amendments through a petition process. The process requires: (1) a petition committee of five registered electors files with the city; (2) signatures equal to 10% of voters in the last general municipal election are collected within one year; (3) the Franklin County Board of Elections validates the signatures; (4) the amendment appears on a ballot for a simple majority vote.

**Q6: How many signatures are needed?**
Based on approximately 220,000 voters in the last general municipal (mayoral) election, we need roughly 22,000 valid signatures. We will confirm the exact threshold when we formalize the petition committee with the Franklin County Board of Elections.

**Q7: When would the vote happen?**
Once validated signatures are submitted, the amendment would appear on the next eligible ballot — either a primary or general election. The goal is to build public support and gather signatures on a timeline that leads to a vote within two to three years.

**Q8: Does the City Council have to approve this?**
No. A citizen-initiated charter amendment bypasses City Council entirely. It goes directly from petition signatures to the ballot. Council could separately place a similar measure on the ballot, but our path does not require their cooperation.

**Q9: What if the vote fails?**
Then the name stays Columbus, and the democratic process has spoken. We believe in the process regardless of the outcome. A robust public conversation about the city's identity has value even if the measure does not pass on the first attempt.

### Cost and Logistics

**Q10: How much would renaming cost?**
Honest answer: we do not have a precise figure yet, and anyone who claims to is guessing. The cost depends on implementation decisions — phased vs. immediate, which systems change first, how the transition is managed. Conservative estimates for a city this size range from tens of millions spread over multiple years. We are commissioning an independent cost analysis and will publish it. We believe a phased transition — replacing signs at end-of-life, updating digital systems in bulk, maintaining dual-name recognition during transition — significantly reduces the sticker shock.

**Q11: Who pays for it?**
City government would bear some costs (signage, official documents, systems). State and federal entities would update their own records on normal cycles. Private businesses would update on their own timelines. A phased approach over 3–5 years distributes these costs and allows them to overlap with normal replacement cycles.

**Q12: What about the sports teams? Ohio State?**
The Columbus Crew, Columbus Blue Jackets, and other institutions would make their own decisions about branding. History suggests most would adopt the new name — as happened when teams in renamed cities worldwide adjusted. Ohio State University is "The Ohio State University" — its identity is not dependent on the city's name. These conversations would happen during the transition period.

### History and Identity

**Q13: Wasn't Columbus just a man of his time?**
Columbus was imprisoned by the Spanish Crown — his own employers — for the brutality of his governance. He was extreme even by the standards of his era. But our core argument does not depend on whether Columbus was uniquely bad. Our argument is that he has no connection to this city. Whether you view him as villain or man-of-his-time, he never set foot in Ohio. The name is borrowed, not earned.

**Q14: Isn't this erasing history?**
Renaming a city is not erasing history — it is making a different choice about what to honor. The history of Columbus, Ohio, does not disappear. Every book, record, photograph, and memory remains. What changes is the name on the signs, which shifts from honoring a distant historical figure to describing the place itself. We support robust historical education about the city's full past — including the naming, the Indigenous history, and the 210 years of life under the Columbus name.

**Q15: What about Italian American heritage?**
We take this concern seriously. Columbus Day was in part a recognition of Italian American contributions during an era of anti-Italian discrimination. But Italian American heritage in Columbus is real and specific — the North End markets, the community institutions, the families who built this city. That heritage deserves its own dedicated recognition, not a borrowed connection to a Genoese explorer. We support creating dedicated Italian American heritage spaces and recognition as part of any renaming process.

**Q16: What do Indigenous communities think?**
We are actively engaging with Indigenous communities and organizations in central Ohio. We do not speak for them. The Indigenous history at the confluence — the Shawnee, Delaware, Mingo, and Wyandot presence, the nearly 200 mounds that once existed in Franklin County — is a crucial part of this story, and Indigenous voices must be central to how it is told.

**Q17: Are there other cities named Confluence?**
There is a small borough called Confluence in Somerset County, Pennsylvania (population roughly 750), also named for a river confluence. No major American city carries the name.

### Getting Involved

**Q18: How can I help?**
Sign the petition. Share the campaign with friends. Volunteer — we need signature collectors, social media amplifiers, event organizers, neighborhood captains, and content creators. Donate to fund the campaign's operations. Every action moves us closer to the ballot.

**Q19: I'm not sure how I feel about this. Is that okay?**
Absolutely. We built the Voices section of this site specifically for people working through this question. We welcome supporters, opponents, and everyone in between. Read the arguments, explore the history, and share your perspective. This should be a conversation, not a loyalty test.

**Q20: I oppose renaming. Will my voice be heard here?**
Yes. Our Voices section publishes perspectives from all positions — support, oppose, and undecided. We believe the strongest case for renaming is made in the presence of the strongest case against it. If you have a thoughtful perspective, we want to publish it.

**The city is already asking this question.** "Columbus removed the statue. Columbus replaced the holiday. The city invested $3.5 million to 'reimagine' its relationship with the name. We are simply following that conversation to its natural conclusion."
