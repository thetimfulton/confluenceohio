# Confluence Ohio — Historical Research Reference

## Verified Facts (with sources)

### The Founding of Columbus, Ohio
- Founded February 14, 1812
- Originally favored name: "Ohio City" — renamed Columbus before incorporation
- Joseph Foos: Franklinton tavern owner and state legislator who reportedly convinced colleagues (legend says over drinks) to name the capital after Christopher Columbus
- Founding document describes the location as "High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto"
- Source: Ohio History Central, Columbus Metropolitan Library archives

### The Rivers
- Scioto River: 231 miles, longest river flowing entirely within Ohio, appears on state seal
- "Scioto" — from the Wyandot language, likely meaning "deer" (reflecting abundant wildlife along its banks; also translated as "hairy river" for the same reason)
- Olentangy River: 97 miles
- Original name: "Whetstone River" — from the Delaware (Lenape) word keenhongsheconsepung meaning "place of the sharp tool"
- "Olentangy" means "river of the red face paint" in Delaware (Lenape) — but this name originally belonged to a DIFFERENT waterway (likely Big Darby Creek, where red ochre was found). The Ohio General Assembly accidentally reassigned the name to the Whetstone River in 1833 during an effort to restore Indigenous place names. This is a documented legislative error.
- The confluence of the Scioto and Olentangy is in northern downtown Columbus, just northwest of present-day North Bank Park
- Source: USGS, Ohio DNR, Wikipedia (Olentangy River, Scioto River)

### Indigenous History at the Confluence
- Shawnee, Delaware (Lenape), Mingo, and Wyandot peoples had presence in the area
- Franklin County once contained nearly 200 burial and ceremonial mounds
- Most have been destroyed; one survives on McKinley Avenue
- The confluence area was a recognized gathering place for centuries before European settlement
- Source: Ohio History Connection, Ohio Exploration Society

### Christopher Columbus — Key Historical Facts
- Four voyages (1492–1504) to the Caribbean, Central America, and northern South America
- Never reached the North American continent — never came close to Ohio
- Second voyage (1493): enslaved an estimated 550 Arawak people; ~200 died en route to Spain
- As governor of Hispaniola: imposed gold tribute quotas; those who failed had hands cut off
- Forced mining labor killed roughly one-third of the native workforce every six months
- Spanish Crown sent Francisco de Bobadilla to arrest Columbus in 1500; he was returned to Spain in chains
- Contemporary witness: Bartolomé de Las Casas documented conditions in "A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies" (1552)
- Source: Las Casas primary accounts, Columbus's own letters and logs, Bobadilla's report to the Spanish Crown

### Columbus, Ohio's Own Actions
- July 1, 2020: Mayor Andrew Ginther ordered removal of 20-foot Christopher Columbus statue from City Hall (erected 1955). Cost ~$90,000. Confirmed it will not be reinstalled.
- 2018–2020: City stopped celebrating Columbus Day, transitioned to Indigenous Peoples' Day (Ohio state later created dual designation)
- June 2023: "Reimagining Columbus" initiative launched — $3.5M total ($2M Mellon Foundation Monuments Project + $1.5M City of Columbus). Runs through June 2025.
- Source: NPR, WOSU, Mellon Foundation

### City Renaming Precedents
- Utqiaġvik, Alaska (formerly Barrow): voted October 10, 2016 (381–375), effective December 1, 2016. Iñupiaq name meaning "place for gathering wild roots."
- Cincinnati, Ohio (formerly Losantiville): renamed January 4, 1790 by Governor Arthur St. Clair. Named for the Society of the Cincinnati.
- Atlanta, Georgia: Terminus (1837) → Marthasville (1843) → Atlanta (1845)
- St. Paul, Minnesota: informally "Pig's Eye Landing" (1830s–40s) after Pierre Parrant; renamed after St. Paul chapel, incorporated 1849
- Mumbai, India (formerly Bombay): officially changed March 1995, effective March 1996. Shiv Sena government, honoring goddess Mumbadevi.
- Source: NPR, Minnesota Historical Society, Georgia Public Broadcasting, American Name Society

### The Flavortown Petition
- 2020 Change.org petition to rename Columbus "Flavortown" after Guy Fieri
- Collected over 117,000 signatures
- Fieri publicly distanced himself from the effort
- Demonstrated genuine public appetite for reconsidering the city's name
- Source: Change.org, media coverage

### Legal Path for Renaming
- Columbus City Charter, Section 42: citizen-initiated charter amendments
- Requires: petition committee of 5 registered electors; signatures from 10% of voters in last general municipal (mayoral) election (~12,533 based on 2025 turnout of 125,329); one-year collection window; Franklin County Board of Elections validation; simple majority at next eligible election
- Does not require City Council approval
- Source: Columbus City Charter

### Key Statistics
- Columbus population (city proper): ~905,000 (14th largest US city)
- Metro area: ~2.2 million
- Columbus area code: 614
- Places named for Christopher Columbus in the US: 23 cities named "Columbus" plus 60+ additional towns/counties with Columbus/Columbia; broader count of 6,000+ includes streets, buildings, schools, monuments, etc.
- Source: US Census Bureau, World Population Review, Monument Lab

## Corrections and Cautions

These claims from the original research summary required correction or clarification:

1. **Scioto meaning**: Confirmed as Wyandot for "deer." The meaning "deer" is one common translation; "hairy" (referring to deer abundance) is another.

2. **Olentangy name**: The plan correctly notes the 1833 error but should specify: the name originally belonged to a different waterway (likely Big Darby Creek), and the river was previously called "Whetstone" from the Delaware keenhongsheconsepung ("place of the sharp tool").

3. **Flavortown signatures**: The plan says "118,000+" — verified count is "over 117,000." Close enough for general use but should not claim 118K+ specifically.

4. **"6,000+ US places named for Columbus"**: This is accurate only if you count streets, buildings, schools, monuments, etc. There are ~23 cities actually named "Columbus." Specify which count you mean.

5. **Columbus "never came within a thousand miles"**: Columbus never reached North America AT ALL, so the "thousand miles" framing actually understates the distance. More accurate: "never set foot on the North American continent."

6. **Population**: ~905,000 city proper (2024 estimate), so "900K+" is accurate. Use ~905,000 for specificity.
