## 3. The Case: History — `/the-case/history`

### Hero

**Headline:** How Columbus Got Its Name

**Subhead:** A tavern, a borrowed name, and a city that almost had a different one.

---

### Before Columbus: The Land at the Forks

Long before any European name was attached to this place, the land at the confluence of the Scioto and Olentangy rivers was home to Indigenous peoples for thousands of years. The Shawnee, Delaware (Lenape), Mingo, and Wyandot all had presence in what is now central Ohio. Franklin County once held nearly 200 burial and ceremonial mounds — earthworks that speak to centuries of spiritual and cultural significance at this meeting of waters. Most have since been destroyed, though one survives on McKinley Avenue.

The rivers drew people here. They have been doing so for a very long time.

---

### Franklinton: The First Settlement

In 1797 — fifteen years before Columbus existed — a surveyor named Lucas Sullivant founded Franklinton on the west bank of the Scioto River, naming it after Benjamin Franklin. It became the seat of Franklin County and the region's first permanent European-American settlement.

Franklinton looked east across the Scioto to the high bluffs on the opposite bank — a ridge the early settlers called **Wolf's Ridge**, for the eastern gray wolves that still roamed the area. The last confirmed wolf sighting in the region came in 1842.

When Ohio's legislature decided in 1810 to build a permanent state capital, Franklinton's residents expected the new city would be theirs. Instead, the legislature chose the other side of the river — the high ground across the Scioto, at the forks where the Olentangy joined it. The founding document describes the chosen site as "the High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto."

The rivers chose the location. But who chose the name?

---

### Joseph Foos and the Naming

The new capital needed a name. The legislators initially favored "Ohio City" — a functional, geographic choice. But a Franklinton tavern owner and state legislator named Joseph Foos had a different idea.

Foos admired Christopher Columbus, as many Americans did in the early 19th century. Columbus was widely celebrated in the young republic as a symbol of exploration, discovery, and the New World. Columbus's name was already on the District of Columbia, dozens of towns, and even a song ("Hail, Columbia"). He was, in 1812, an uncontroversial hero.

Legend holds that Foos lobbied his colleagues over drinks at his tavern — a story that may be apocryphal but has persisted for two centuries. What we know for certain is that the legislature chose "Columbus" over "Ohio City," and the capital was formally established on February 14, 1812.

It was a fine name for its time. The question we are asking is whether it still is.

---

### The Name in Context

Why did early Americans love Christopher Columbus? Because they needed a founding myth that predated England. Columbus — Italian by birth, Spanish by employ, and connected to no single colonial power — offered a convenient symbol of discovery and independence. Washington Irving's romanticized 1828 biography cemented the mythology. Columbus Day became a federal holiday in 1937, driven in part by Italian American advocacy for recognition during an era of intense anti-Italian discrimination.

The mythology began to crack in the late 20th century as historians returned to primary sources: Columbus's own letters, the accounts of Bartolomé de Las Casas, the records of the Spanish colonial administration. What they found was more complicated — and more troubling — than the schoolbook version.

Columbus, the city, has already started grappling with this. In 2020, the statue came down. The holiday was replaced. The Reimagining Columbus initiative invested $3.5 million to rethink how the city tells its own story. The name is the question that remains.

---

### Timeline

- **~1000 BCE – 1700s CE:** Indigenous peoples establish settlements and ceremonial sites at the Scioto-Olentangy confluence. Nearly 200 mounds built in Franklin County.
- **1797:** Lucas Sullivant founds Franklinton on the west bank of the Scioto.
- **1803:** Ohio becomes a state. Chillicothe serves as the first capital.
- **1810:** Legislature authorizes a permanent capital at the "High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto."
- **1812:** Capital formally established on February 14. Named "Columbus" over "Ohio City," reportedly at the urging of Joseph Foos.
- **1816:** Columbus becomes the official state capital.
- **1833:** Ohio General Assembly accidentally renames Whetstone Creek to "Olentangy" — a Delaware name that actually belonged to a different waterway.
- **1937:** Columbus Day becomes a federal holiday.
- **2020:** Columbus statue removed from City Hall (July 1). City transitions to Indigenous Peoples' Day.
- **2023:** "Reimagining Columbus" initiative launches ($3.5M, Mellon Foundation + City).
- **2026:** Confluence Ohio launches petition campaign.

## 4. The Case: The Rivers — `/the-case/the-rivers`

### Hero

**Headline:** The Confluence

**Subhead:** Two rivers meet in the heart of this city. That meeting is why the city exists.

---

### The Scioto

The Scioto River flows 231 miles from its headwaters near Kenton, Ohio, south through the center of the state to the Ohio River at Portsmouth. It is the longest river flowing entirely within Ohio's borders — significant enough to appear on the state seal.

Its name comes from the Wyandot language, likely meaning "deer" — a reflection of the abundant wildlife that once lined its banks. The Scioto is Ohio's river: it threads through the state's geographic and political center, past the capital, carrying water that has shaped settlement patterns for thousands of years.

In downtown Columbus, the Scioto runs through the heart of the city. The Scioto Mile — a network of parks, paths, and gathering spaces along the riverfront — has transformed what was once a neglected waterway into one of the city's most beloved public spaces. On any given weekend, you will find runners on the path, families in Bicentennial Park, and kayakers on the water.

---

### The Olentangy

The Olentangy River runs 97 miles from its source in Crawford County south through Delaware County and the Columbus metro, winding past Ohio State's campus before joining the Scioto.

Its name has a curious history. The settlers called it the Whetstone, from the Delaware (Lenape) word *keenhongsheconsepung* — "place of the sharp tool," likely referring to the flint deposits along its banks. In 1833, the Ohio General Assembly tried to restore Indigenous place names to several waterways. In the process, they accidentally assigned the name "Olentangy" — a Delaware word meaning "river of the red face paint," after the red ochre found along its banks — to the wrong river. The name actually belonged to what is likely Big Darby Creek. The mix-up stuck.

It is a small irony: even the river's name is a case of mistaken identity. But the river itself is no mistake. The Olentangy Greenway Trail runs 13 miles along its banks, connecting neighborhoods from Worthington to downtown, passing through some of the most used park land in the city.

---

### Where They Meet

Just northwest of North Bank Park, in the northern edge of downtown Columbus, the Olentangy flows into the Scioto. This is the confluence.

It is not dramatic in the way some river junctions are — no crashing whitewater, no towering canyon walls. It is a quiet meeting of two Midwestern rivers, visible from the bridges and paths that cross this part of the city. Confluence Park, a 21-acre green space, sits at the junction. The Boat House restaurant overlooks it. The Olentangy Trail ends here; the Scioto Trail begins.

But this quiet junction is the reason Columbus exists. When the Ohio legislature chose a capital site in 1812, they chose the high banks at the forks of the Scioto — this specific spot, where the rivers come together. The geography determined the city. The confluence came first.

---

### A Place of Gathering — Before and After 1812

The rivers drew people here long before European settlement. The Shawnee, Delaware, Mingo, and Wyandot peoples all recognized the confluence as a gathering point. The nearly 200 mounds that once dotted Franklin County — burial sites, ceremonial earthworks, markers of civic and spiritual life — cluster along the river corridors. The rivers were not just water sources. They were routes, boundaries, meeting places.

That has not changed. The Scioto Mile and the Olentangy Trail are where Columbus gathers now. The riverfront festivals, the morning jogs, the Saturday walks along the water — these are the modern expressions of the same geographic pull that brought people to this spot for millennia.

---

### The Metaphor That Is Not a Metaphor

"Confluence" is not just a geographic term. It is the word for what happens in this city every day.

Columbus is one of the most diverse metropolitan areas in the Midwest. It is home to one of the largest Somali communities in the United States — an estimated 60,000 to 70,000 people, with over 500 Somali-owned businesses. Significant Nepali and Bhutanese refugee communities have established roots here. The city's Appalachian, African American, and Latin American communities are woven into its neighborhoods from Linden to the Hilltop. Ohio State University brings 60,000 students from around the world.

This is a city where state government and startup culture converge, where logistics hubs and research labs sit side by side, where a Big Ten university campus meets the Short North arts district meets a revitalizing Franklinton. It is a city defined by what comes together here.

"Confluence" is not a metaphor applied to this city. It is a description of what this city is.

## 5. The Case: Columbus Legacy — `/the-case/columbus-legacy`

### Hero

**Headline:** Who Was Christopher Columbus?

**Subhead:** The documented record — and why the city that bears his name has already started to reckon with it.

---

### The Mythology

For most of American history, Christopher Columbus was presented as a hero: the bold explorer who "discovered" the New World in 1492. This version of Columbus — the visionary who proved the Earth was round and opened a new continent to civilization — was taught in schools, celebrated in a federal holiday, and honored in the names of cities, counties, and the nation's capital district.

The real history is more complicated.

---

### The Documented Record

Columbus made four voyages between 1492 and 1504, reaching Caribbean islands, the coasts of Central America, and northern South America. He never reached the North American continent — never set foot in what would become the United States, and certainly never came anywhere near Ohio.

What he did in the places he reached is documented in Spanish colonial records, his own letters, and the accounts of contemporaries:

**Enslavement.** On his second voyage in 1493, Columbus enslaved an estimated 550 Arawak people. Roughly 200 died during the transatlantic crossing to Spain.

**Forced labor and brutality.** As governor of Hispaniola, Columbus imposed a gold tribute system on the Indigenous Taino people. Those who failed to meet quotas had their hands cut off. Forced labor in mines killed roughly one-third of the native workforce every six months, according to contemporary estimates.

**Arrest by Spain.** Columbus's governance was so extreme that the Spanish Crown sent Francisco de Bobadilla to investigate in 1500. Bobadilla arrested Columbus and shipped him back to Spain in chains.

**Contemporary testimony.** Bartolomé de Las Casas, a Spanish priest who witnessed the aftermath of Columbus's governance, documented the conditions in *A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies* (1552). His accounts describe systematic destruction of entire peoples.

These are not revisionist claims. They are documented in primary sources that historians have studied for centuries. The mythology of Columbus as a benign explorer is a relatively modern invention.

---

### Zero Connection to Ohio

This point is simple but worth emphasizing: Christopher Columbus never came to Ohio. He never came to North America. His four voyages took him to the Caribbean and the coasts of Central and South America. He had no connection to the Scioto River, no knowledge of the Olentangy, no awareness that the confluence existed.

The name "Columbus" was attached to this city in 1812 as a tribute to a figure who was widely admired at the time. It was a borrowed name — not a name earned through connection to this place.

---

### What the City Has Already Done

Columbus, Ohio has not waited for a renaming campaign to begin grappling with its namesake:

**July 1, 2020:** Mayor Andrew Ginther ordered the removal of a 20-foot Christopher Columbus statue from the grounds of City Hall. The statue, donated by the city's Italian American community in 1955, was placed in storage at a cost of approximately $90,000. The city has confirmed it will not be reinstalled.

**2018–2020:** The city stopped officially celebrating Columbus Day, transitioning to recognition of Indigenous Peoples' Day.

**June 2023:** The city launched the **Reimagining Columbus** initiative — a $3.5 million project funded by the Mellon Foundation's Monuments Project ($2 million) and the City of Columbus ($1.5 million). The initiative ran through June 2025 and focused on rethinking the space where the statue stood and the broader question of how the city tells its own story.

When a city removes the statue, replaces the holiday, and spends $3.5 million reimagining the narrative — the name is the remaining question.

---

### A Note on Italian American Heritage

We want to address this directly. For many Italian Americans, Christopher Columbus has historically represented a point of cultural pride and visibility. Columbus Day was established partly as recognition of Italian American contributions during an era of severe anti-Italian discrimination. The statue at City Hall was donated by the Italian American community in 1955.

We understand that renaming can feel like erasure. It is not our intention. Italian American contributions to Columbus are real and specific — from the markets of the old North End to the families and institutions that helped build this city. That heritage deserves recognition on its own terms, not through a figure whose legacy is increasingly contested.

We support dedicated Italian American heritage recognition — a cultural center, a heritage trail, named public spaces — that celebrates the community's actual contributions to this city.

## 6. The Case: Precedents — `/the-case/precedents`

### Hero

**Headline:** Cities That Changed Their Names

**Subhead:** It has happened before. It was disruptive, and then it was normal.

---

### Introduction

City renaming sounds radical until you look at how many cities have done it. Across the United States and around the world, communities have changed their names when the old name no longer fit — or when a better one was waiting. In every case, the city survived. In most cases, it thrived.

---

### Cincinnati, Ohio (1790) — Ohio's Own Precedent

**Before:** Losantiville
**After:** Cincinnati
**When:** January 4, 1790

Right here in Ohio, the settlement that would become Cincinnati was originally named Losantiville — a tortured neologism coined by surveyor John Filson, who patched together Latin and French roots meaning "the town opposite the mouth of the Licking River." Even by 18th-century naming standards, it was a mouthful.

Governor Arthur St. Clair renamed it Cincinnati in 1790, after the Society of the Cincinnati, an organization of Revolutionary War officers named for the Roman citizen-soldier Cincinnatus. The new name stuck. Cincinnati became one of the great American cities of the 19th century.

Ohio has renamed a major city before. The process worked. The city is fine.

---

### Utqiaġvik, Alaska (2016) — The Most Recent Example

**Before:** Barrow
**After:** Utqiaġvik
**When:** October 10, 2016 (effective December 1, 2016)

In 2016, residents of Barrow, Alaska — the northernmost city in the United States — voted 381 to 375 to restore the city's original Iñupiaq name, Utqiaġvik, meaning "place for gathering wild roots." The margin was just six votes.

The campaign was driven by a desire to reclaim Indigenous identity and move past a colonial-era name (the city had been named for a British naval officer). The transition was not without controversy — a margin that narrow ensures some lasting disagreement. But the name changed, official records were updated, and the city moved on.

Utqiaġvik is the most recent American city renaming — and a reminder that it can be done at any scale, by democratic vote.

---

### Atlanta, Georgia (1845) — The Triple Rename

**Before:** Terminus (1837), then Marthasville (1843)
**After:** Atlanta (1845)

The city that became Atlanta was founded in 1837 as Terminus — literally "the end of the line," because it was where the Western & Atlantic Railroad ended. In 1843, it was renamed Marthasville after the governor's daughter. Just two years later, in 1845, it was renamed again to Atlanta, reportedly suggested by a railroad engineer who derived it from "Atlantica-Pacifica."

Atlanta changed its name twice in eight years. Today it is one of the most recognized city names in the world. No one mourns the loss of "Terminus."

---

### St. Paul, Minnesota (1849) — From Pig's Eye to Sainthood

**Before:** Pig's Eye Landing (1830s–1840s)
**After:** St. Paul (formally incorporated 1849)

In the 1830s, the settlement that would become Minnesota's capital was informally called "Pig's Eye Landing" after Pierre Parrant, a one-eyed French-Canadian fur trader who operated a tavern on the riverbank. When a Catholic priest named Lucien Galtier built a chapel and named it for St. Paul in the early 1840s, the settlement followed suit.

The parallel to Columbus is hard to miss: a tavern owner gave one city its name, and a tavern owner's legacy got replaced in another. Sometimes tavern names age well. Sometimes they don't.

---

### Mumbai, India (1995–96) — Renaming at Global Scale

**Before:** Bombay
**After:** Mumbai (officially March 1995, effective March 1996)

Mumbai is the case that answers the biggest objection: "But everyone knows the old name." Bombay was one of the most recognized city names on earth. When India's Maharashtra state government officially renamed it Mumbai — after the goddess Mumbadevi, reclaiming the Marathi heritage from a colonial-era English name — critics predicted confusion, economic damage, and brand destruction.

None of that happened. Mumbai is now the world's fourth-largest metropolitan area. Its film industry is still called "Bollywood." International businesses did not flee. The name change took time to propagate, but within a decade, "Mumbai" was standard usage worldwide.

If a city of 20 million can change its name without losing its identity, a city of 905,000 can too.

---

### Others Worth Knowing

**Denali / Mount McKinley (2015):** The federal government officially restored the name Denali to North America's tallest peak, ending decades of Alaskan advocacy. The state of Ohio, ironically, was the primary opponent — because President William McKinley was from Canton, Ohio.

**Istanbul / Constantinople (1930):** Turkey officially adopted the name Istanbul — which had been in common Turkish usage for centuries — as part of broader national modernization. The city's global recognition only grew.

**Chennai / Madras (1996):** India renamed Madras to Chennai the same year as Mumbai's change, also to reclaim Indigenous heritage over colonial naming.

---

### The Pattern

Successful city renamings share common features:

1. **A clear reason** — geographic, cultural, or democratic
2. **A better name waiting** — not just opposition to the old one
3. **Democratic process** — a vote, a legal mechanism, a community decision
4. **A transition period** — dual-name usage, phased sign replacement, patience
5. **Time** — within a few years, the new name becomes normal

Columbus has all five ingredients.

## 7. The Case: The Process — `/the-case/the-process`

### Hero

**Headline:** How It Works

**Subhead:** 22,000 signatures. A ballot measure. A simple majority. Here is the path to Confluence.

---

### Overview

Renaming Columbus does not require an act of Congress or permission from City Council. Ohio law gives citizens the power to propose charter amendments directly — through a petition that, if validated, places the question on the ballot for voters to decide.

This is democracy working exactly as designed.

---

### The Five Steps

**Step 1: Form a Petition Committee**
Five registered Columbus electors file a petition committee with the city. This committee is the legal sponsor of the charter amendment. *(Done.)*

**Step 2: Collect Signatures**
The committee has up to two years to collect valid signatures from Columbus registered voters equal to 10% of the votes cast in the last general municipal election — approximately **22,000 signatures**. Signatures must be from Franklin County voters, collected in person, written in ink.

**Step 3: Submit to the Board of Elections**
The completed petition is submitted to the Franklin County Board of Elections for signature validation. The City Clerk and City Attorney also review the petition for legal sufficiency.

**Step 4: Validation and Certification**
The Board of Elections verifies that enough valid signatures have been collected. If the threshold is met, the charter amendment is certified for the ballot.

**Step 5: Voters Decide**
The charter amendment appears on the next eligible ballot — a primary or general election. A **simple majority** (50% + 1) of voters approves the change. No supermajority. No City Council veto. The voters decide.

---

### Where We Are Now

Every signature is verified for Ohio residency before it counts. We are building this petition the right way — no shortcuts, no padding.

---

### Legal Authority

This process is governed by **Section 42 of the Columbus City Charter**, which establishes the right of citizens to propose charter amendments by petition. The amendment process is further supported by the Ohio Revised Code provisions for municipal charter amendments.

The charter amendment would modify the official name of the city in the charter document. All subsequent legal, administrative, and public-facing references would update on a transition timeline established by the amendment's language.

---

### Frequently Asked Questions About the Process

**Does City Council have to approve this?**
No. A citizen-initiated charter amendment bypasses City Council entirely. It goes from petition to ballot to voters. Council could separately place a similar measure on the ballot, but our path does not require their cooperation.

**What if we do not reach 22,000 signatures?**
Then the measure does not go to the ballot, and the name stays Columbus. We believe we will reach the threshold.

**What if the vote fails?**
Then the name stays Columbus, and the democratic process has spoken. We believe in the process regardless of the outcome.

**What would the transition look like?**
The charter amendment would include a phased transition plan: immediate updates to digital systems and official documents, gradual replacement of physical signage over 3–5 years (aligning with normal replacement cycles), and a dual-name recognition period. This is how Mumbai, Utqiaġvik, and other cities have managed their transitions.

**How much would it cost?**
We are commissioning an independent cost analysis and will publish the results. A phased transition spread over several years — replacing signs at end-of-life, updating digital systems in bulk — significantly reduces the total cost compared to an immediate switchover.

## 14. FAQ — `/faq`

*(The complete 20 Q&As from Artifact 01, Section 7. Displayed in an accordion format grouped by category. Full text reproduced below for the MDX file.)*

### The Basics

**What is Confluence Ohio?**
Confluence Ohio is a 501(c)(4) civic organization building a movement to rename Columbus, Ohio to Confluence, Ohio — replacing a name honoring a historical figure with no connection to this place with one rooted in the geography that created the city. The Scioto and Olentangy rivers meet in downtown Columbus; that confluence is why the city exists.

**Why "Confluence" specifically?**
Confluence is the geographic term for the meeting of two rivers — which is exactly what happens in downtown Columbus. The Scioto and Olentangy converge just northwest of North Bank Park. This is the physical feature that determined the city's location when the state legislature chose it as the capital in 1812. The word also captures the city's identity as a place where diverse communities, industries, and ideas come together. No other major American city carries this name.

**Is this a joke, like the Flavortown petition?**
No. The 2020 Flavortown petition — which collected over 117,000 signatures — demonstrated that tens of thousands of people were genuinely open to rethinking the city's name. We channel that energy into a substantive proposal: a name rooted in geography and history, pursued through the legal process Ohio provides.

**Who is behind this campaign?**
Confluence Ohio is a 501(c)(4) civic organization led by Columbus residents. We are transparent about our team, our funding, and our methods. See our About page for details.

### The Process

**How would this actually happen legally?**
Under Columbus's city charter, citizens can propose charter amendments through a petition process. The process requires: (1) a petition committee of five registered electors files with the city; (2) signatures equal to 10% of voters in the last general municipal election are collected within the allowed timeframe; (3) the Franklin County Board of Elections validates the signatures; (4) the amendment appears on a ballot for a simple majority vote.

**How many signatures are needed?**
Based on approximately 220,000 voters in the last general municipal election, we need roughly 22,000 valid signatures. We will confirm the exact threshold when we formalize the petition committee with the Franklin County Board of Elections.

**When would the vote happen?**
Once validated signatures are submitted, the amendment would appear on the next eligible ballot — either a primary or general election. The goal is to build public support and gather signatures on a timeline that leads to a vote within two to three years.

**Does the City Council have to approve this?**
No. A citizen-initiated charter amendment bypasses City Council entirely. It goes directly from petition signatures to the ballot. Council could separately place a similar measure on the ballot, but our path does not require their cooperation.

**What if the vote fails?**
Then the name stays Columbus, and the democratic process has spoken. We believe in the process regardless of the outcome. A robust public conversation about the city's identity has value even if the measure does not pass on the first attempt.

### Cost and Logistics

**How much would renaming cost?**
Honest answer: we do not have a precise figure yet, and anyone who claims to is guessing. The cost depends on implementation decisions — phased vs. immediate, which systems change first, how the transition is managed. We are commissioning an independent cost analysis and will publish it. We believe a phased transition — replacing signs at end-of-life, updating digital systems in bulk, maintaining dual-name recognition during transition — significantly reduces the sticker shock.

**Who pays for it?**
City government would bear some costs (signage, official documents, systems). State and federal entities would update their own records on normal cycles. Private businesses would update on their own timelines. A phased approach over 3–5 years distributes these costs and allows them to overlap with normal replacement cycles.

**What about the sports teams? Ohio State?**
The Columbus Crew, Columbus Blue Jackets, and other institutions would make their own decisions about branding. History suggests most would adopt the new name — as happened when teams in renamed cities worldwide adjusted. Ohio State University is "The Ohio State University" — its identity is not dependent on the city's name. These conversations would happen during the transition period.

### History and Identity

**Wasn't Columbus just a man of his time?**
Columbus was imprisoned by the Spanish Crown — his own employers — for the brutality of his governance. He was extreme even by the standards of his era. But our core argument does not depend on whether Columbus was uniquely bad. Our argument is that he has no connection to this city. Whether you view him as villain or man-of-his-time, he never set foot in Ohio. The name is borrowed, not earned.

**Isn't this erasing history?**
Renaming a city is not erasing history — it is making a different choice about what to honor. The history of Columbus, Ohio, does not disappear. Every book, record, photograph, and memory remains. What changes is the name on the signs, which shifts from honoring a distant historical figure to describing the place itself. We support robust historical education about the city's full past — including the naming, the Indigenous history, and the 210 years of life under the Columbus name.

**What about Italian American heritage?**
We take this concern seriously. Columbus Day was in part a recognition of Italian American contributions during an era of anti-Italian discrimination. But Italian American heritage in Columbus is real and specific — the North End markets, the community institutions, the families who built this city. That heritage deserves its own dedicated recognition, not a borrowed connection to a Genoese explorer. We support creating dedicated Italian American heritage spaces and recognition as part of any renaming process.

**What do Indigenous communities think?**
We are actively engaging with Indigenous communities and organizations in central Ohio. We do not speak for them. The Indigenous history at the confluence — the Shawnee, Delaware, Mingo, and Wyandot presence, the nearly 200 mounds that once existed in Franklin County — is a crucial part of this story, and Indigenous voices must be central to how it is told.

**Are there other cities named Confluence?**
There is a small borough called Confluence in Somerset County, Pennsylvania (population roughly 750), also named for a river confluence. No major American city carries the name.

### Getting Involved

**How can I help?**
Sign the petition. Share the campaign with friends. Volunteer — we need signature collectors, social media amplifiers, event organizers, neighborhood captains, and content creators. Donate to fund the campaign's operations. Every action moves us closer to the ballot.

**I'm not sure how I feel about this. Is that okay?**
Absolutely. We built the Voices section of this site specifically for people working through this question. We welcome supporters, opponents, and everyone in between. Read the arguments, explore the history, and share your perspective. This should be a conversation, not a loyalty test.

**I oppose renaming. Will my voice be heard here?**
Yes. Our Voices section publishes perspectives from all positions — support, oppose, and undecided. We believe the strongest case for renaming is made in the presence of the strongest case against it. If you have a thoughtful perspective, we want to publish it.

### Body

**Two rivers meet in the heart of this city.**

The Scioto — 231 miles long, the longest river flowing entirely in Ohio — flows south through the center of the state. The Olentangy — 97 miles, its very name a product of a legislative mix-up in 1833 — winds down past Ohio State, through parks and neighborhoods, before joining the Scioto just northwest of North Bank Park.

That meeting of waters is why Columbus exists. When the Ohio legislature picked a site for the state capital in 1812, they chose specifically the "High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto." The rivers came first. The city followed.

Today, we are launching a campaign to give this city a name that reflects that truth.

**Confluence Ohio** is a 501(c)(4) civic organization building a movement to rename Columbus, Ohio to **Confluence, Ohio**. We are using the democratic process that Ohio law provides — a citizen petition that, with 22,000 valid signatures, puts a charter amendment on the ballot for voters to decide by simple majority.

This is not a prank. This is not a protest. This is a serious civic proposal, pursued through the legal channels available to every Ohio citizen.

---

**Why now?**

Because the city itself has been asking this question for years.

In 2020, Columbus removed the Christopher Columbus statue from City Hall. The same year, the city stopped celebrating Columbus Day, replacing it with Indigenous Peoples' Day. In 2023, the city invested $3.5 million — $2 million from the Mellon Foundation, $1.5 million from city funds — in the "Reimagining Columbus" initiative, a project dedicated to rethinking the city's relationship with its namesake.

We did not start this conversation. The city did. We are following it to what we believe is its natural conclusion: if the statue is gone, the holiday is gone, and $3.5 million has been spent reimagining the legacy — maybe the name itself is worth reconsidering.

---

**Why "Confluence"?**

Because it is literally what this city is.

"Confluence" is the word for the meeting of two rivers. It is also a word for the meeting of peoples, ideas, cultures, and industries — which is what happens in Columbus every day. This city is one of the most diverse in the Midwest, home to one of the largest Somali communities in America, significant Nepali and Bhutanese populations, historic Appalachian and African American communities, and a Latin American presence that is growing every year. It is where Ohio State's research enterprise meets one of the country's largest logistics sectors. It is a state capital, a college town, and a tech hub simultaneously.

"Columbus" tells you about a 15th-century Italian mariner who never set foot on this continent. "Confluence" tells you about the place itself.

No other major American city carries the name. It is distinctive, immediately evocative, and carries only positive connotations: coming together, meeting, convergence.

---

**What we are not.**

We are not here to mock people who love the name Columbus. Two centuries of lived experience under that name carry real weight. For many residents, "Columbus" means home — Saturday mornings in German Village, Friday nights at the stadium, the feeling of telling someone "I'm from Columbus" and watching them nod in recognition. We respect that.

We are not here to reduce this to "Columbus was bad, therefore change the name." The historical record on Christopher Columbus is troubling — the enslavement, the brutality, the arrest by his own employers — but our core argument is simpler. Columbus, the explorer, has no connection to Columbus, the city. He never came here. The name was borrowed in 1812 by a tavern-owning legislator named Joseph Foos who admired a then-popular historical figure. It was a fine name for its time.

We are asking whether it is still the right name for this time.

---

**What happens next.**

We are collecting signatures. Ohio law requires approximately 22,000 valid signatures from Columbus voters to put a charter amendment on the ballot. Every signature is verified for Ohio residency. When we reach the threshold, the Franklin County Board of Elections validates the petition, and the question goes to voters.

A simple majority decides. No City Council approval needed. No backroom deals. Just voters deciding the name of their city.

You can share your perspective — whether you support, oppose, or are undecided — at [confluenceohio.org/voices](https://confluenceohio.org/voices).

You can volunteer, donate, or just follow along as this conversation unfolds.

The rivers are still meeting. They have been meeting since long before 1812. We think the name should say so.
