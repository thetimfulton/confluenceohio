**Two rivers meet in the heart of this city.**

The Scioto — 231 miles long, the longest river flowing entirely in Ohio — flows south through the center of the state. The Olentangy — 97 miles, its very name a product of a legislative mix-up in 1833 — winds down past Ohio State, through parks and neighborhoods, before joining the Scioto just northwest of North Bank Park.

That meeting of waters is why Columbus exists. When the Ohio legislature picked a site for the state capital in 1812, they chose specifically the "High Banks opposite Franklinton at the Forks of the Scioto." The rivers came first. The city followed.

Today, we are launching a campaign to give this city a name that reflects that truth.

**Confluence Ohio** is a 501(c)(4) civic organization building a movement to rename Columbus, Ohio to **Confluence, Ohio**. We are using the democratic process that Ohio law provides — a citizen petition that, with 22,000 valid signatures, puts a charter amendment on the ballot for voters to decide by simple majority.

This is not a prank. This is not a protest. This is a serious civic proposal, pursued through the legal channels available to every Ohio citizen.

---

**Why now?**

Because the city itself has been asking this question for years.

In 2020, Columbus removed the Christopher Columbus statue from City Hall. The same year, the city stopped celebrating Columbus Day, replacing it with Indigenous Peoples' Day. In 2023, the city invested $3.5 million — $2 million from the Mellon Foundation, $1.5 million from city funds — in the "Reimagining Columbus" initiative, a project dedicated to rethinking the city's relationship with its namesake.

We did not start this conversation. The city did. We are following it to what we believe is its natural conclusion: if the statue is gone, the holiday is gone, and $3.5 million has been spent reimagining the legacy — maybe the name itself is worth reconsidering.

---

**Why "Confluence"?**

Because it is literally what this city is.

"Confluence" is the word for the meeting of two rivers. It is also a word for the meeting of peoples, ideas, cultures, and industries — which is what happens in Columbus every day. This city is one of the most diverse in the Midwest, home to one of the largest Somali communities in America, significant Nepali and Bhutanese populations, historic Appalachian and African American communities, and a Latin American presence that is growing every year. It is where Ohio State's research enterprise meets one of the country's largest logistics sectors. It is a state capital, a college town, and a tech hub simultaneously.

"Columbus" tells you about a 15th-century Italian mariner who never set foot on this continent. "Confluence" tells you about the place itself.

No other major American city carries the name. It is distinctive, immediately evocative, and carries only positive connotations: coming together, meeting, convergence.

---

**What we are not.**

We are not here to mock people who love the name Columbus. Two centuries of lived experience under that name carry real weight. For many residents, "Columbus" means home — Saturday mornings in German Village, Friday nights at the stadium, the feeling of telling someone "I'm from Columbus" and watching them nod in recognition. We respect that.

We are not here to reduce this to "Columbus was bad, therefore change the name." The historical record on Christopher Columbus is troubling — the enslavement, the brutality, the arrest by his own employers — but our core argument is simpler. Columbus, the explorer, has no connection to Columbus, the city. He never came here. The name was borrowed in 1812 by a tavern-owning legislator named Joseph Foos who admired a then-popular historical figure. It was a fine name for its time.

We are asking whether it is still the right name for this time.

---

**What happens next.**

We are collecting signatures. Ohio law requires approximately 22,000 valid signatures from Columbus voters to put a charter amendment on the ballot. Every signature is verified for Ohio residency. When we reach the threshold, the Franklin County Board of Elections validates the petition, and the question goes to voters.

A simple majority decides. No City Council approval needed. No backroom deals. Just voters deciding the name of their city.

The rivers are still meeting. They have been meeting since long before 1812. We think the name should say so.
